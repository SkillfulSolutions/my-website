My Home Technology Path

The Original Goal

I strongly dislike subscription services. Recent years with Hulu, Amazon Prime, Netflix, Disney, and HBO show that, despite rising fees, service quality declines. Advertisements intrude on content we've paid for, access to certain media is lost, or it moves to other services, requiring multiple subscriptions. The only improvement is convenience.

Having such a perspective, under fair use, I've relied on third-party streaming sites that don't charge fees, though copyright holders have increasingly targeted these sites, degrading their quality and impacting convenience—my three-year-old now knows the term 'buffering.'

My initial goal in self-hosting my media library was somewhat unclear: I wanted more than just external hard drives with file directories, but I was hesitant to return to peer-to-peer file sharing after nearly a decade away. File sharing carries additional risks, and technology has become quite complex. As a boy, I didn't fully understand what I was doing while downloading software from random sources on Napster and Limewire, cracking software or using key generators —I just wanted to play Age of Empires! (all of this is completely hypothetical; I would never do something so illegal and irresponsible!)

Eventually, I found myself in an online echo chamber of digital anarchists where I learned about Linux, open-source software and self-hosting. These topics resonated with my concerns about digital privacy and data leaks involving my family.

How It's Meandered

The digital anarchists recommended a software suite that simplified media access, organization, and sharing—everything I sought for my family. This software was best installed on Linux, and I had been considering switching from Windows. I began converting my desktop gaming PC into a home media server, installed Linux Mint Debian Edition (LMDE) on my laptop, and researched home server operating systems. My interest in scalability, customization, and unfamiliarity with Terminals led me to Unraid, a user-friendly proprietary OS based on Slackware Linux. Within a year, I had started numerous projects, feeling overwhelmed by the array of unfinished hardware scattered around me.

Within the year, I had initiated enough projects that I felt like a golden retriever in a tennis ball court. Everything seemed so logical at the time, but I look around and see miscellaneous hardware from barely-begun or half-complete projects.

Where I'm At

Current hardware and functions:

  • Tower: My Unraid server is the heart of my home network, with 16TB HDD for media storage and 4TB NVMe for caching. With 64GB of DDR4 RAM, it's running multiple Docker containers, although many are currently dormant.
  • Hypervisor: My Proxmox server has exceeded my expectations, providing a flexible environment for running virtual machines. It's primarily used for school projects and testing software.
  • Synology DiskStation: My 12-bay Synology DiskStation is a powerful storage solution that I plan to configure for backups and media storage. The small NVMe cache drive enhances performance, but I need to update the firmware first.
  • Laptop: My laptop serves as my primary workstation, where I manage personal notes and projects using Obsidian. It's lightweight and portable, making it perfect for studying on the go. It's running Linux Mint Debian Edition.
  • Desktop: My desktop is a refurbished HP ProDesk 600 G5 MT. It's just a backup workstation and to test things. It's running Linux Mint Debian Edition.
  • Firewall box: My dedicated firewall box is a HP T740 Thin Client. Once I configure it, it will ensure that my home network remains secure from external threats and facilitate network segregation for personal devices, guest devices and smart-home devices.
  • N100 Mini-pc: My Beelink EQ12 N100 mini-PC is perfect for lightweight tasks and serves as an energy-efficient machine for additional containers.
  • RaspberryPi Zero: This ran DietPi and hosted my Pi-Hole DNS sinkhole for ad-blocking, until I accidentally reset the power when I was stretching. I had configured it early in my learning process so I'll probably reinstall the OS from scratch and re-configure.
  • Samsung Galaxy Tab A9: Once I configure Immich on my Unraid server, this device will act as a self-hosted digital photo frame. I want a digital photo frame, but I don't want to share my photos or network access with an additional corporation if I can avoid it.
  • Too many consumer routers: Routers for wifi access points, routers to act as a switch and bridge to the primary network, routers for travel so I can have VPN on all devices in hotels.

Cloud services and function:

  • Codeberg: My Remote repository is for personal notes accessed through Obsidian.
  • Cloud server: *cough* *cough* server space in a jurisdiction that is neutral to media copyrights and the DCMA.

I've continued to run into some technical skill roadblocks, and they seem to bring me back to the same thing: I need to learn more.
